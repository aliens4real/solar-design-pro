1. How Solar PV Systems Work

The Photovoltaic Effect

Solar panels (modules) convert sunlight directly into electricity through the photovoltaic effect. Each module contains dozens of silicon cells. When photons from sunlight hit a cell, they knock electrons free from atoms in the silicon, creating a flow of electric current. This current is direct current (DC) — it flows in one direction, like a battery.

Key facts about module output:

  • A typical residential module produces 400–450 watts of DC power under ideal conditions (STC: 1000 W/m² irradiance, 25°C cell temperature)
  • Real-world output is usually 75–85% of STC rating due to temperature, soiling, shading, and other losses
  • Output voltage per module is approximately 30–50 VDC (open-circuit voltage, Voc)
  • Modules produce power whenever sunlight hits them — they cannot be "turned off" like a light switch
⚡ Critical Safety Concept

DC voltage is present on module wiring any time the sun is up. Even on cloudy days, modules produce significant voltage. Always treat module wiring as energized during daylight hours.

String Inverter vs. Microinverter Architecture

The two most common residential configurations:

String Inverter (SMA Sunny Boy / Fronius Primo GEN24)

Multiple modules are wired in series (positive-to-negative, like batteries in a flashlight) to form a "string." The string's DC voltage adds up — for example, 12 modules at ~40V each = ~480V DC. This high-voltage DC string runs through conduit down to a single centralized inverter mounted on the wall near the main panel. The inverter converts all the DC to 240V AC.

Module─Module─Module─Module─Module─Module (String 1: ~240V DC) ┌──────────────────────────────────────┘ │ ┌──────────┐ ┌───────┐ ┌───────┐ └────────▶│ STRING │────▶│ AC │────▶│ MAIN │ │ INVERTER │ AC │DISCON │ │ PANEL │ ┌────────▶│(wall mt) │ └───────┘ └───────┘ │ └──────────┘ └──────────────────────────────────────┐ Module─Module─Module─Module─Module─Module (String 2: ~240V DC)

Figure 1-2: String inverter — modules in series, one central inverter

  • Pros: Lower cost, fewer components, easier troubleshooting
  • Cons: Shade on one module affects the whole string; requires external rapid shutdown devices (NEC 690.12)

Microinverter (Enphase IQ8)

Each module has its own small inverter mounted underneath it. DC-to-AC conversion happens at the module level. The microinverters connect to a pre-wired Q Cable trunk that carries 240V AC down to an IQ Combiner or IQ Gateway near the panel.

  • Cons: Higher cost per watt, more components on roof, Q Cable routing

Net Metering

Most residential solar systems are grid-tied with net metering. When the system produces more power than the home uses, excess electricity flows backward through the meter to the grid, and the homeowner receives a credit. At night or during cloudy periods, the home draws power from the grid normally. The customer pays only for their net consumption.

2. Safety First

⚡ #1 Rule of Solar Installation

Solar modules produce DC electricity whenever exposed to light. You cannot de-energize them during daylight. A typical residential string can produce 300–600 VDC — enough to cause serious injury or death. Respect the voltage at all times.

Electrical Hazards

  • DC shock risk: DC voltage is always present on exposed module leads and MC4 connectors when the sun is up. Even 30V DC can be dangerous in wet conditions.
  • Arc flash: High-voltage DC arcs are sustained and extremely hot. Unlike AC, DC arcs do not self-extinguish at zero-crossing. Never disconnect DC connectors under load.
  • AC hazards: When working near the main panel, service entrance, or inverter AC side, 240V AC is present. Always de-energize AC circuits at the breaker before making connections.
  • Ground faults: Damaged insulation, pinched wires, or water intrusion can create ground fault paths through your body. Use insulated tools and gloves.

Fall Protection & Roof Safety

Falls from height are the leading cause of death in construction. OSHA requires fall protection at 6 feet or more in construction (29 CFR 1926.501).

  • Harness: Full-body harness with dorsal D-ring, properly fitted and inspected before each use
  • Anchor: Roof anchor rated for 5,000 lbs, fastened to structural member (rafter or truss)
  • Lanyard/rope grab: Self-retracting lifeline (SRL) or rope grab system. Max free-fall distance: 6 feet.
  • Ladder safety: 3-point contact at all times, extend 3 feet above roof edge, secure at top and bottom, 4:1 pitch ratio
  • Roof conditions: Check for wet/icy surfaces, loose shingles, rotted decking, skylights, and power lines before stepping on the roof
  • Perimeter awareness: Never work within 6 feet of an unprotected roof edge without fall protection engaged

Lockout/Tagout (LOTO)

Before making any electrical connection on the AC side:

  1. Turn OFF the AC disconnect switch
  2. Turn OFF the solar breaker at the main panel
  3. Apply your personal lock and tag to both disconnects
  4. Verify zero energy with a multimeter (test L1-N, L2-N, L1-L2)
  5. Only then proceed with wiring
⚠️ DC Cannot Be Locked Out

There is no way to fully lock out DC from solar modules during daylight. Cover modules with opaque tarps if you must work on DC wiring, or wait until dark. Even covered modules may produce residual voltage.

Heat Illness Prevention

Solar installation often means working on hot roofs in direct sun:

  • Hydrate: Drink water every 15–20 minutes, even if not thirsty. Minimum 1 quart per hour in hot conditions.
  • Shade breaks: 10-minute shade break every hour when temperatures exceed 85°F
  • Buddy system: Watch crew members for signs of heat exhaustion: heavy sweating, weakness, nausea, dizziness, fast pulse
  • Acclimatization: New workers need a 2-week ramp-up period for hot conditions
  • Roof temperature: Comp shingle roofs can reach 150–170°F on sunny days. Wear knee pads and avoid prolonged kneeling.

Tool Safety

  • Inspect power tools before each use — check cords, guards, bits
  • Use the right tool for the job — never use a screwdriver as a pry bar
  • Secure tools when on the roof — a dropped impact driver can injure someone below
  • Keep cutting tools sharp — dull blades cause more injuries than sharp ones
  • Never carry tools while climbing a ladder — use a tool belt or haul bucket

Emergency Procedures

  1. Electrical shock: Do NOT touch the victim while they're in contact with the source. Disconnect power if possible. Call 911. Begin CPR if the victim is unresponsive.
  2. Fall: Do not move the victim (possible spinal injury). Call 911. Keep them calm and still.
  3. Fire: DC-related fires cannot be extinguished with water. Use a Class C or ABC extinguisher. Evacuate and call the fire department.
  4. Severe weather: Lightning = immediately descend from roof. Monitor weather forecasts before starting work.

4. Pre-Installation

Site Assessment Review

Before showing up to install, review the design package from Solar Design Pro. Know the answers to these questions:

  • Roof type: Comp shingle? Tile? Metal? (Determines attachment hardware)
  • Roof pitch: Affects safety requirements and racking approach
  • Shade: Any obstructions — trees, vents, chimneys, neighboring structures?
  • Structural: Rafter spacing (typically 16" or 24" o.c.), rafter size, any engineering concerns?
  • Electrical: Main panel location, service size (100A/200A), available breaker spaces, meter location
  • Access: Where can we place ladders? Driveway access for truck? Gate codes?
  • Permit conditions: Any special requirements from the building department?

Reading the Installation Plan

The Solar Design Pro output includes:

  1. Module layout — exact panel positions on the roof, orientation (portrait/landscape), number of panels per row
  2. String configuration — which modules are wired together, string voltage and current calculations
  3. Electrical elevation diagram — shows equipment placement, conduit routing, wire sizes, and connections
  4. Pack list (BOM) — complete material list with quantities for every component
💡 Pro Tip

Print the module layout and electrical elevation diagram. Tape them inside your truck toolbox lid for quick reference throughout the day. A laminated copy on the roof saves trips up and down the ladder.

Material Staging & Truck Loading

Load the truck the afternoon before, using the pack list as your checklist:

Loading Order (bottom to top of truck bed)

  1. Rails (heaviest, longest items — lay flat on bottom, extend over tailgate if needed, flag the ends)
  2. Modules (stack flat on foam, never on edge, max 20 per stack per manufacturer guidelines)
  3. Conduit (bundle with strap, 10-ft sticks)
  4. Racking hardware (FlashFoot2s, clamps, bolts — in labeled bins)
  5. Electrical (wire spools, disconnect, roof box, inverter — box separate)
  6. Tools & safety gear (accessible on top)

Verifying the BOM

At the shop, before loading:

  • Count rails — correct lengths and quantity?
  • Count FlashFoot2s — matches pack list?
  • Count mid clamps + end clamps — correct for panel count?
  • Verify wire spools — correct gauge and sufficient length?
  • Conduit sticks — enough for all runs?
  • Fittings bag — connectors, locknuts, bushings, straps, LBs?
  • Inverter — correct model? Verify nameplate.
  • Disconnect — correct amperage rating?
  • Roof box (SolaDeck) — correct model for voltage? Terminal blocks inside?
  • Grounding — ground rods, acorn clamps, grounding wire spool?

Pre-Job Safety Briefing

Every morning, before anyone climbs a ladder, the crew lead conducts a 5-minute safety briefing:

  1. Identify the day's hazards (roof pitch, electrical, weather, obstacles)
  2. Review fall protection plan — anchor locations, harness inspection
  3. Assign roles (who's on roof, who's ground support)
  4. Confirm emergency contacts and nearest hospital
  5. Weather check — any rain, lightning, or extreme heat expected?

Roof Access & Ladder Setup

  • Set up the extension ladder on firm, level ground. Use leg levelers if needed.
  • Extend at least 3 feet above the roof edge (3 rungs)
  • Angle: 4:1 ratio (for every 4 feet of height, the base is 1 foot from the wall)
  • Secure the top with a ladder stabilizer or tie-off strap
  • Secure the base — stake it, have someone foot it, or use anti-slip pads
  • First person up installs the roof anchor and connects their fall protection immediately

5. Racking Installation

The racking system is the structural foundation for every solar array. Proper installation is critical for safety, waterproofing, and long-term performance. Canopy Solar primarily uses IronRidge XR100 rails for residential and XR1000 rails for commercial and ground mount.

5.1 Comp Shingle Residential (FlashFoot2 + XR100)

This is the most common install type. The IronRidge FlashFoot2 is an integrated flashing + L-foot that attaches the rail to the roof through the sheathing and into the rafter below.

Step 1: Find and Mark Rafters

  1. Use a magnetic stud finder on the roof surface to locate rafters (the magnet picks up nails in the rafter)
  2. Confirm rafter spacing — typically 16" or 24" on center
  3. Mark rafter centerlines with chalk or a pencil. Snap a chalk line along each rafter that will receive an attachment point.
  4. Verify by probing with a small drill bit through the shingle if unsure — you must hit solid wood
⚡ Never Miss a Rafter

Every FlashFoot2 lag bolt MUST hit a rafter or engineered truss member. Lag bolts into sheathing only (no rafter) have virtually zero pullout strength. A "toggle bolt" or "molly bolt" is NOT an acceptable substitute. If you miss a rafter, remove the lag, seal the hole, and find the correct rafter location.

Step 2: Layout FlashFoot2 Positions

  1. Refer to the design plan for rail positions and attachment spacing
  2. Mark FlashFoot2 positions along each rail line, centered on rafters
  3. Typical spacing: 48"–72" between FlashFoot2s on XR100 (per IronRidge Design Assistant output)
  4. Maximum overhang past the last FlashFoot2: no more than 6 inches of rail past the last attachment
  5. Rail spacing between top and bottom rails: approximately 2/3 of the module's long dimension in portrait orientation (e.g., ~67" for a standard 2102mm module)

Step 3: Install FlashFoot2s

  1. Lift the shingle course with a flat bar
  2. Slide the FlashFoot2 flashing under the upper shingle course, centered on the rafter mark
  3. The L-foot points upward, perpendicular to the roof surface
  4. Drive the 5/16" x 4" stainless steel lag bolt (included) into the rafter using an impact driver with a 5/16" hex socket. The lag must penetrate a minimum of 2.5 inches into the rafter.
  5. Verify the FlashFoot2 is snug against the roof and the flashing is sealed under the shingles
  6. Apply sealant (included) over the bolt head and under any exposed flashing edges

Step 4: Install Rails

  1. Lift rails to the roof using a rope/pulley or hand-over-hand with a helper
  2. Set the rail on the FlashFoot2 L-feet, seating it in the channel
  3. Slide a T-bolt + flange nut into the rail channel at each L-foot
  4. Loosely tighten — enough to hold but still allow adjustment
  5. Align rails using a chalk line or string line for straightness
  6. Square the array — measure diagonals (they should be equal)
  7. Final tighten all T-bolts to 20 ft-lbs (240 in-lbs)

Step 5: Splice Rails (if needed)

When a row of panels is longer than a single rail, use a BOSS Splice to join two rails end-to-end:

  1. Slide the BOSS Splice into the T-slot channel, bridging the gap between two rail ends
  2. Center the splice at the joint — equal amount inside each rail
  3. The BOSS Splice is a bonded structural connection — it maintains full load capacity and electrical bonding

Step 6: Install Grounding Lugs

  1. Slide a rail grounding lug (XR-LUG-03-A1) into the T-slot of each rail (or each continuous rail run)
  2. Position near the end of the rail closest to the conduit run to the ground rod
  3. Torque the lug bolt per IronRidge specs
  4. Later, you'll connect the grounding electrode conductor (#6 AWG bare copper) to these lugs

5.2 Flat Roof Commercial

Flat (low-slope) commercial roofs use either ballasted or mechanically attached tilt systems.

Ballasted System (IronRidge BX or Similar)

  • No roof penetrations — the array is held in place by weight (concrete ballast blocks)
  • A chassis frame sets modules at 5° or 10° tilt facing south
  • Ballast blocks (typically CMU blocks or poured concrete pans) are placed per the engineering layout
  • Weight varies by wind zone: 3–5 lbs/sq ft of array area is typical
  • Requires a structural engineer to verify the roof can handle the added dead load
  • Rubber pads protect the roof membrane from abrasion

Attached Tilt Mount (IronRidge XR Tilt)

  • When roof can't handle ballast weight or wind loads are too high, mechanically fasten to the roof deck
  • Uses XR1000 rails on fixed tilt legs
  • Tilt legs adjust from 5°–30° depending on latitude
  • Attachments penetrate the roof membrane — requires proper flashing and sealant

5.3 Ground Mount

The IronRidge XR Ground Mount system uses XR1000 rails on locally sourced Schedule 40 steel pipe.

Assembly Sequence

  1. Set post spacing per design: typically 7.5' or 9' north-south between posts
  2. Drive/set posts plumb using a post level
  3. Install Top Caps (cast aluminum, 2" or 3" pipe) on each post — these accept the cross-pipe
  4. Install horizontal cross-pipes (Schedule 40 steel) through the Top Caps
  5. Mount XR1000 rails to the cross-pipes using the provided hardware
  6. Install tilt legs to set the desired array angle (typically latitude tilt: 20°–35°)
  7. Square and level the array
  8. Install modules with UFO clamps (same process as roof mount)

5.4 Carport

Carport solar uses a steel canopy structure (typically W-beams or HSS tube steel columns with C-purlins) with rails mounted to the purlins.

  • Steel structure is engineered and fabricated separately — not an IronRidge product
  • Purlins (C-channel or Z-channel) run perpendicular to the modules
  • Mount XR100 or XR10 rails to purlins using L-feet + bolts through purlins
  • Module installation and clamping is the same as roof mount
  • Typical carport height: 12–15 ft clearance. Scissor lift or scaffolding required.

5.5 Clamp Installation (All Mount Types)

UFO Mid Clamps

Secure two adjacent modules where they meet on the rail. Quantity: 2 × (N − 1) per row where N is the number of panels.

  1. Place the UFO clamp in the rail channel between two module frames
  2. The clamp straddles both frame edges
  3. Thread the integrated bolt down, engaging the T-slot
  4. Torque to 80 in-lbs (use a torque wrench — do NOT over-tighten by feel)
  5. The UFO clamp has integrated UL 2703 grounding — the serrated contact surface bonds each module frame to the rail, eliminating the need for separate WEEB clips

EFO End Clamps (or UFO End Clamps)

Secure the outer edge of the first and last panel in each row. Quantity: 4 per row (2 on each rail).

  1. Slide the end clamp into the rail channel at the outer edge of the end module
  2. Position so it grips the module frame from the outside
  3. Torque to 80 in-lbs

End Stoppers

Small stops that slide into the rail slot at each end to prevent hardware from sliding off. Match to module frame thickness (30–40mm). Quantity: 4 per row.

6. Module Installation

Handling & Transport

  • Modules are fragile glass — handle with care, avoid impacts, drops, and flexing
  • Never set a module face-down on gravel, concrete, or rough surfaces — micro-cracks in the cells reduce output permanently
  • Store modules flat on foam or cardboard. Stack no more than 20 modules per manufacturer guidelines.
  • When carrying, grip the frame edges — never carry by the junction box or cables
  • Use two people for modules over 45 lbs (most modern modules are 50–60 lbs)
  • On steep roofs, stage modules on the rail immediately — never lean them against anything unsecured
⚠️ Module Handling

Visible cracks or chips in the glass require module replacement. Even hairline cell cracks (invisible to the eye) degrade performance. Handle gently — this is expensive glass.

Orientation: Portrait vs. Landscape

OrientationDescriptionWhen Used
PortraitLong side vertical, short side along the railMost residential — simpler layout, fewer rails
LandscapeLong side horizontal, short side verticalLow-slope commercial, height-constrained areas

Always install in the orientation specified by the design plan. Mixing orientations affects string voltage calculations.

Setting Modules on Rails

  1. Start at one end of the row — typically the hardest-to-reach end first (farthest from ladder)
  2. Place the module frame edges onto both rails, within the clamp zone marks
  3. Slide the module against the end stopper or the previously installed module
  4. Install the appropriate clamp:
    • First module: Install an end clamp on the outside edge of both rails
    • Subsequent modules: Install a mid clamp between the new module and the previous one on both rails
    • Last module: Install the second end clamp on the outside edge of both rails
  5. Torque each clamp to 80 in-lbs as you go

WEEB Grounding Clips

If using older (non-UFO) clamps that do not have integrated grounding, install a WEEB grounding clip (serrated bonding washer) at each clamp location. The WEEB bites through the anodized coating on both the module frame and the rail, creating an electrical bond.

With current-generation UFO and EFO clamps, the integrated grounding pin eliminates the need for separate WEEB clips — the clamp itself provides UL 2703-listed bonding when torqued to spec.

MC4 Connectors

Each module has two pigtail cables with MC4 connectors (one positive, one negative). These snap together to form series strings.

  1. Route the MC4 cables under the modules, along the rail channel
  2. Connect the positive lead of one module to the negative lead of the next (series connection)
  3. Push the MC4 male and female connectors together firmly until you hear/feel a click
  4. Tug-test each connection — a properly mated MC4 cannot be pulled apart by hand (requires the disconnect tool)
  5. Avoid excess cable hanging below the array — use wire management clips
⚡ MC4 Safety

Never connect or disconnect MC4 connectors under load. MC4 connectors are NOT load-break rated. Disconnecting under load causes arcing that damages the connector and creates a fire hazard. Always verify zero current (cover modules or wait until dark) before separating MC4s.

Wire Management

  • Install wire management clips (IronRidge DCS-1307-5000) in the rail T-slot every 3–4 feet
  • Bundle MC4 cables and home-run conductors together neatly
  • NEC 690.31 requires PV source wiring to be secured and supported
  • Keep cables away from sharp edges and roof surfaces — no sagging or rubbing
  • Leave enough slack at each connector for thermal expansion and module removal if needed

Module-Level Rapid Shutdown (MLRSD)

NEC 690.12 requires that PV conductors within the array boundary be reduced to 80V or less within 30 seconds of initiating shutdown. How this is achieved depends on the inverter type:

7. Electrical — Conduit & Wiring

This section covers EMT (Electrical Metallic Tubing) conduit installation, wiring, and the SolaDeck roof box. All work must comply with the National Electrical Code (NEC) and local amendments.

7.1 EMT Conduit Basics

EMT is thin-wall steel conduit — the most common raceway for solar installations. It comes in 10-foot sticks and standard trade sizes (1/2", 3/4", 1", 1-1/4").

Cutting

  1. Measure and mark the cut line with a pencil
  2. Cut with a pipe cutter, hacksaw, or angle grinder with a metal cut-off wheel
  3. Deburr inside and outside — a reamer or round file removes the sharp edge that could damage wire insulation (NEC 358.28)

Bending

Use a hand bender matched to the conduit size. Common bends:

Bend TypeDescriptionWhen Used
90° bendQuarter-circle turnGoing from horizontal to vertical (or vice versa)
OffsetTwo opposing bends to shift conduit sidewaysTransitioning around obstacles or into an enclosure
Kick (10°–30°)Shallow bend to adjust angleGetting conduit flush against a wall or transitioning slope
SaddleUp-over-down to clear an obstructionCrossing another conduit or pipe
📏 NEC 358.24 — Bend Limits

No more than 360° of total bends (equivalent to four 90° bends) between pull points. If you need more bends, install an LB fitting or pull box at the 360° limit and start a new section.

Key NEC Rules for EMT Support (358.30)

  • Strap conduit within 3 feet of every box, fitting, or enclosure
  • Support conduit at intervals not exceeding 10 feet
  • Use one-hole straps — the strap count formula: ceil(run_length / 10) + 1

7.2 Conduit Fittings

7.3 Pulling Wire Through Conduit

  1. Select wire: THWN-2 (copper) for conduit runs. Gauge per design (typically #10 AWG for residential up to ~30A, #8 or #6 for larger systems)
  2. Check conduit fill: NEC allows max 40% fill for 3+ conductors. Verify wire cross-sections don't exceed conduit capacity.
  3. Fish tape: Push a fish tape or pull string through the conduit from one end
  4. Attach wires: Strip ends, bend through the fish tape loop, and tape smooth
  5. Lubricate: Apply wire-pulling lubricant for runs over 20 feet — it dramatically reduces friction
  6. Pull together: One person feeds wire at the entry, one pulls at the exit. Pull steadily — don't jerk.
  7. Leave slack: Leave 8–12 inches of wire extending from each box for terminations

7.4 SolaDeck Roof Box

The SolaDeck 0799-5B is Canopy Solar's standard roof box — a NEMA 3R enclosure that provides the transition point between rooftop PV wiring and conduit. It's mounted directly to the roof deck, with integrated flashing.

Installation

  1. Position the SolaDeck on the roof between the array and the conduit route to the inverter
  2. Lift shingles and slide the flashing underneath, same as a vent boot
  3. Fasten to the roof deck with the provided screws (hit at least one rafter if possible)
  4. Seal all edges with roofing sealant
  5. Route PV wires from the array into the top of the box (through knockouts or cable glands)
  6. Attach EMT conduit to the bottom/side knockouts for the run down to the inverter

Interior Wiring (String Inverter)

Inside the SolaDeck, wires transition from PV cable (exposed rooftop wiring) to THWN-2 conductors (in conduit). Use DIN-rail-mounted terminal blocks:

  • 2 terminal blocks — Black for DC+ and Red for DC−
  • 1 grounding terminal block (green/yellow) for the equipment grounding conductor (EGC)
  • 2 end stops on the DIN rail ends
  • 2 end covers between terminal block sections

7.5 Wire Terminations

  • Strip wire to the correct length for the terminal (typically 5/8" to 3/4")
  • Insert into the terminal block or breaker terminal
  • Torque to the manufacturer's specification (see Appendix)
  • Aluminum wire: Apply anti-oxidant compound (Noalox or equivalent) before inserting into the terminal. Aluminum oxidizes quickly and causes high-resistance connections.
  • Multi-strand wire: Use a compression lug or terminal rated for the wire type — never insert bare stranded wire into a setscrew terminal rated only for solid wire

The equipment grounding conductor (EGC) provides a low-impedance fault-current path back to the source:

  • All metallic enclosures (disconnect, roof box, junction boxes) must be bonded

8. Inverter Installation

8.1 String Inverter — SMA Sunny Boy / Fronius Primo GEN24

Mounting

  1. Select location: shaded exterior wall near the main panel (north-facing wall is ideal to minimize heat exposure)
  2. Minimum clearances: 12" on each side, 12" above, 36" below for ventilation and service access
  3. Mount the wall bracket (included) using appropriate fasteners:
    • Wood stud: 5/16" × 3" lag bolts (hit 2 studs minimum)
    • Masonry/stucco: 3/8" concrete sleeve anchors
  4. Hang the inverter on the bracket and secure the bottom locking screws

DC Connections

  1. Route the DC home-run MC4 cables from the roof box / conduit to the inverter location
  2. String inverters have MC4 pigtails for DC input — simply mate the MC4 connectors from the strings to the inverter pigtails
  3. Verify polarity: positive to positive, negative to negative
  4. Measure open-circuit voltage (Voc) on each string with a multimeter before connecting:
    • Expected Voc = (number of modules in string) × (module Voc at temperature)
    • Must be within the inverter's MPPT voltage range
    • Must NOT exceed the inverter's maximum DC input voltage

AC Connections

  1. Open the inverter's wiring compartment (bottom cover)
  2. Route THWN-2 conductors from the conduit into the AC terminal block
  3. Typical connections: L1 (black), L2 (red), Neutral (white), Ground (green or bare)
  4. Wire size: #10–#6 AWG depending on inverter output current and distance
  5. Torque all terminal screws to the inverter manufacturer's specification

Conduit Entries

SMA and Fronius have bottom-entry knockouts:

  • 1× 3/4"–1" knockout for AC conduit
  • 1× 1/2" knockout for communication wiring
  • DC enters via MC4 cable glands (no conduit needed for DC on string inverters)

Communication Setup

  • SMA: Production CTs (included) install around the AC conductors inside the inverter. Connect WiFi or Ethernet for SMA Sunny Portal monitoring. Consumption CTs sold separately (~$50/pair).
  • Fronius: DataManager built-in. Fronius Smart Meter (~$250) needed for consumption monitoring and export limiting. WiFi or Ethernet connection.

8.2 Microinverter — Enphase IQ8

Under-Module Mounting

  1. Each IQ8 microinverter mounts to the underside of the module frame or directly to the racking rail using the provided bracket
  2. Position the microinverter near the module's junction box for short DC lead routing
  3. Connect the module's MC4 leads to the microinverter's DC input MC4 connectors
  4. Connect the microinverter's AC output to the Q Cable trunk connector (twist-lock, pre-wired)

Q Cable Routing

  • Q Cable is a pre-wired trunk cable with twist-lock connectors spaced every ~5 feet (one per module position)
  • Route the Q Cable along the rail, under the modules
  • Secure with wire clips every 3–4 feet
  • At the end of each branch, install a Q Cable Terminator (~$5) to seal the unused end
  • Route the Q Cable's home-run end into the SolaDeck roof box, then transition to THWN-2 in conduit for the run to the IQ Combiner

IQ Gateway / IQ Combiner Installation

The IQ Combiner 4 (~$628) is the central hub that combines all microinverter branch circuits and provides the AC disconnect function:

  1. Mount the IQ Combiner on the wall near the main panel (same location where a string inverter would go)
  2. Route AC conduit from the roof box into the Combiner
  3. The Combiner has a main breaker that serves as the AC disconnect — no separate disconnect needed
  4. Route AC output from the Combiner to the main panel via conduit
  5. Connect communication: the IQ Gateway communicates with microinverters via power-line communication (PLC) — no separate communication wiring to the roof
  6. Connect WiFi or Ethernet for cloud monitoring via the Enphase app

8.3 AC Disconnect

A visible, lockable AC disconnect is required for all solar systems. It allows the utility and fire department to de-energize the AC side of the system.

  • Location: Near the inverter or near the utility meter — check AHJ requirements
  • Rating: Must be rated for the system's maximum AC output current (typically 60A or 100A disconnect for residential)
  • Type: Non-fused safety switch (e.g., Eaton DG222URB for 60A, DG223URB for 100A) unless fusing is required
  • Wiring: Line side from inverter, load side to panel. Use the same wire gauge throughout (no upsizing or downsizing through the disconnect).
  • Enphase exception: If using an IQ Combiner with a main breaker, the Combiner IS the disconnect

8.4 Commissioning

  1. Verify DC: Measure Voc on each string — compare to calculated value. If significantly low, check for reversed polarity, disconnected modules, or shading.
  2. Verify AC: Close the AC disconnect. Energize the main panel breaker. Check for 240V AC at the inverter.
  3. Start the inverter: Most inverters auto-start when both DC and AC are present. Wait 5 minutes for grid synchronization.
  4. Check production: Verify the inverter is producing power (display or app shows watts being generated).
  5. Monitor setup: Connect the inverter or gateway to WiFi and verify the monitoring app shows the system online.

9. Inspection & Commissioning

Pre-Inspection Self-Audit

Before calling for the building department inspection, walk the entire system with this checklist. Fix any issues BEFORE the inspector arrives.

Roof / Racking

  • All FlashFoot2s hit rafters (verify visually — no spinning lag bolts)
  • All clamps torqued to 80 in-lbs (use torque wrench to verify at least 20% of clamps)
  • Rail splices properly seated
  • No module overhang exceeding 6" past last attachment
  • Wire management clips installed, wiring neat and secured
  • Roof penetrations properly flashed and sealed
  • End stoppers installed at all rail ends

Electrical

  • Conduit properly strapped — within 3 ft of every box, every 10 ft thereafter
  • No more than 360° of bends between pull points
  • Connectors, locknuts, and bushings at every conduit entry
  • All terminal connections torqued to spec
  • Wire colors correct: Black (L1), Red (L2), White (Neutral), Green/Bare (Ground)
  • Grounding electrode system complete — 2 ground rods, acorn clamps, GEC connected
  • Bonding bushing installed at service entrance raceway
  • Conduit fill within NEC 40% limit

Labels

  • Main panel label — "WARNING: SOLAR ELECTRIC SYSTEM CONNECTED" (NEC 690.56)
  • Disconnect label — "SOLAR DISCONNECT" with voltage/current ratings
  • Inverter label — AC/DC voltage and current ratings
  • Conduit labels — "SOLAR PHOTOVOLTAIC" on DC conduit at accessible points
  • Module labels — rapid shutdown label near the main service panel (if MLRSD-equipped)
  • Ground rod labels — identified as grounding electrode

  1. Verify DC voltage — Measure Voc on each string at the inverter. Record readings.
  2. Close the AC disconnect
  3. Turn ON the solar breaker at the main panel

  5. Verify AC output — Check power production on the inverter display or monitoring app

Customer Walkthrough

After passing inspection and verifying production:

  1. Show the homeowner where the inverter, disconnect, and monitoring equipment are located
  2. Demonstrate the monitoring app — how to check daily production
  3. Explain the AC disconnect — when and how to use it (emergencies, utility work)
  4. Set expectations: production varies by season, weather, and shade. Winter months produce less.
  5. Provide contact information for any post-installation questions or monitoring alerts
  6. Leave the warranty documents, equipment manuals, and inspection approval in a folder

11. Common Mistakes & Troubleshooting

Installation Mistakes

1. Missed Rafters

What happens: Lag bolt goes into sheathing only (3/8" – 3/4" plywood) — near-zero pullout strength. The FlashFoot2 will eventually pull out in a windstorm.

How to spot it: The lag bolt spins freely or drives in with almost no resistance. Or you can wiggle the L-foot with your hand.

Fix: Remove the lag bolt, seal the hole with roof sealant, and relocate the FlashFoot2 to center on a rafter. Never use toggle bolts, molly bolts, or "creative" solutions.

2. Reverse Polarity on DC Strings

What happens: One module's MC4 connectors are crossed — positive connected to the wrong lead. String voltage reads lower than expected (the reversed module's voltage subtracts from the total).

How to spot it: String Voc is approximately 1 × module Voc lower than calculated. Or the inverter reports a ground fault.

Fix: Cover modules to de-energize. Use MC4 disconnect tool to separate connectors. Trace and correct the reversed connection.

3. Under-Torqued Connections

What happens: Loose electrical connections create high resistance → heat → eventual failure or fire. Loose clamps allow modules to shift in wind.

Fix: Always use a torque wrench for clamps (80 in-lbs) and electrical terminations. "Hand-tight" is not a specification.

4. Missing Knockouts / Bushings

What happens: Open knockouts allow moisture, insects, and debris into enclosures. Missing bushings cause wire insulation damage over time.

Fix: Install a knockout seal on any unused knockout. Install insulating bushings on all conduit entries.

5. Conduit Fill Violations

What happens: Too many wires in too small a conduit. Exceeds NEC 40% fill for 3+ conductors — fails inspection, makes wire pulling difficult, and causes heat buildup.

Fix: Calculate fill before running conduit. When in doubt, upsize the conduit one trade size.

Troubleshooting

Ground Fault Indicator on Inverter

  1. Shut down the system (open AC disconnect, cover modules if possible)
  2. Check all MC4 connections — look for damage, moisture, or exposed wire
  3. Measure insulation resistance between each string conductor and ground using a megger
  4. A reading below 1 MΩ indicates a ground fault — trace the affected string conductor to find the fault location
  5. Common causes: pinched wire, damaged insulation from sharp conduit edge, water in junction box

💡 Pro Tip — Diagnosing String Issues

When troubleshooting a string inverter system, measure Voc of each string at the inverter DC inputs. All strings of the same length should read within ~5V of each other. A string that reads significantly low likely has a reversed, disconnected, or failed module.
